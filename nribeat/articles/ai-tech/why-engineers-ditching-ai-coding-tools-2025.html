Why Smart Engineers Are Ditching AI Coding Tools in 2025

AI scribes and coding assistants promised to transform how engineers work. But as 2025 unfolds, seasoned tech professionals—especially those at US companies—are quietly stepping back from these tools. Not because they don't work, but because what they solve and what they actually cost your career aren't adding up the way vendors promised.

This matters because you're likely making decisions about which best AI coding tools to adopt, and your peers are getting radically different results.

What Is the AI Scribes Backlash and Why Should You Care?

The story goes like this: Early 2024, everyone—from tech leads to individual contributors—rushed to adopt ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and GitHub Copilot. The pitch was simple: let AI handle boilerplate, documentation, and grunt work. You'd get hours back every week.

By mid-2025, quiet disillusionment set in. Engineers began noticing that AI tools didn't just write code—they changed how thinking about code happened. And not always for the better.

Here's the core issue: Using AI for routine tasks doesn't free you for strategic thinking. It atrophies the skills that make you valuable.

A clinical background taught us something applicable to engineering: when you use a tool to handle a cognitive task, you're not just delegating work—you're changing the relationship between you and that work. The document (or code) isn't a by-product. It's part of how you think.

How Indian Tech Professionals Are Using This (and Reconsidering)

The Indian engineer community in the US—scattered across FAANG, startups, and mid-market tech companies—adopted AI tools faster than most. Two reasons: first, competitive pressure (everyone else was using ChatGPT vs Claude vs Gemini). Second, the promise of a productivity edge in already high-pressure roles.

But pattern recognition is catching on:

  • Month 1-3: Massive perceived productivity gain. Code velocity looks great in metrics.
  • Month 4-6: Quality issues emerge. Code is correct but lacks understanding of system architecture. Debug time increases.
  • Month 7+: Awareness that the code isn't yours. Can't explain decisions. Architectural thinking atrophies.

Engineers who stayed disciplined—using AI for scaffolding, not thinking—did fine. Engineers who outsourced cognition found themselves vulnerable when:

  • Performance reviews required demonstrating architectural decision-making
  • System failures demanded deep debugging, not pattern matching
  • Promotions required ownership and systems thinking (skills atrophied by overuse of AI)
  • Job market tightened and differentiation became critical

The software engineer AI impact on salary? Unclear, but trending negative for mid-career engineers who leaned too heavily on tools. Senior engineers who used AI strategically—never surrendering core thinking—saw benefits.

Hands-On: How to Use AI Coding Tools the Right Way (If At All)

If you're using best AI tools 2025, here's the framework that's actually working for thoughtful engineers:

Step 1: Define the Cognitive Boundary

Before opening ChatGPT or Claude, ask: Is this task where I need to develop deeper thinking, or is it genuinely mechanical?

Use AI for:

  • Boilerplate (test setup, config files, repetitive patterns you've already mastered)
  • Syntax lookups (you know the concept, not the exact API)
  • Documentation scaffolding (you refine it, AI doesn't own it)

Don't use AI for:

  • Architecture decisions you don't already understand
  • Debugging unfamiliar codebases (you need that learning)
  • System design during your formative years (ages 0-5 in a role)

Step 2: Verify Everything

AI tools hallucinate. Claude is better than ChatGPT at this. Gemini is somewhere in between. None are perfect. If you use code from any tool without understanding it, you've created a liability.

Command to test: Ask the tool to explain what it wrote. If you can't follow it, don't use it.

Step 3: Stay in the Driver's Seat

The tool should augment your thinking, not replace it. For every line of AI-generated code, you should be able to explain:

  • Why this approach, not another
  • Performance implications
  • Failure modes
  • How it integrates with adjacent systems

If you can't, delete it and write it yourself.

Career Impact: Will This Affect Your Job?

The short answer: Yes. But not how you think.

The software engineer AI impact on salary in 2025 isn't a uniform productivity gain. It's bifurcating the market:

Winners: Engineers who view AI as a footnote to their capabilities. You use it for the 5% of work that's purely mechanical. Your architectural thinking, debugging ability, and system knowledge remain sharp. You become the engineer people want on critical projects.

Losers: Engineers who treated AI as a substitute for skill development. You move fast early but plateau hard. When the job market contracts (which it does, cyclically), you're competing on speed, not judgment. That's a race you can't win—a faster AI will do it better.

The Indian engineering community has a specific advantage here: many of you come from rigorous computer science backgrounds (IIT, BITS, etc.). That foundation is why you're valuable. Don't outsource it to ChatGPT.

Your best bet for 2025-2026: Use AI strategically, stay thoughtful, and treat your core engineering skills like they're your actual job. Because they are.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I stop using AI coding tools completely?

No. But be intentional. Use them for mechanical tasks where you already understand the problem space. Avoid them for learning-adjacent work. The distinction matters more than the tool choice.

Is ChatGPT vs Claude vs Gemini even the real question in 2025?

No. It's about how you use it. Claude is technically stronger at coding, but a mediocre engineer using Claude will outperform a thoughtful engineer using Gemini only if the thoughtful engineer isn't thinking. Don't let tool choice distract from discipline.

Will AI coding tools actually tank my salary growth?

Possibly, if you use them to avoid developing depth. Unlikely if you use them as scaffolding. Your 2025 salary depends less on AI adoption and more on whether you're building irreplaceable judgment—the stuff AI can't do. Stay focused on that.

Bottom Line

The AI scribes that promised to free engineers from tedious work did free some of them. But they also trapped others into thinking that efficiency is the same as value. It isn't.

Your advantage as an Indian engineer in a competitive US tech market is depth, rigor, and systems thinking. Don't accidentally outsource those to a tool. Use AI where it genuinely saves cognitive load. Protect the parts of your brain that make you irreplaceable.

2025 isn't about adopting the best AI tools. It's about knowing which problems are worth your thinking and which aren't. That distinction will determine whether AI makes your career or complicates it.
