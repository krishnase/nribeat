IPL 2025: Why Fair Cricket Isn't Good for Business, Says Muralidaran

📅 April 30, 2026 ⏱ 5 min read ✍️ NRIBeat Editorial Source: ESPN Cricinfo ↗
Disclosure: NRIBeat may earn a commission from links in this article at no extra cost to you. We only recommend services we believe in.

Legendary spinner Muralidaran just dropped a truth bomb that'll make you rethink everything about the IPL: fair wickets and balanced contests? They're actually bad for business in T20 cricket.

In a candid conversation, the cricket icon didn't mince words. "If we give fair wickets, the spectators will say it's become boring because the T20 followers want entertainment, so they want to see the fours and sixes," Muralidaran explained. It's a blunt admission that the IPL, now valued as serious big business, has fundamentally shifted what cricket means in the modern era—and honestly, it changes how we should think about India cricket scores and match outcomes.

The IPL 2025 Business Model: Entertainment Over Fairness

Here's the uncomfortable reality that Muralidaran is highlighting: the IPL isn't just cricket anymore. It's a multibillion-dollar entertainment product competing for eyeballs with Netflix, football, and everything else vying for your attention. When you're operating at that scale, you're not optimizing for fair contests—you're optimizing for engagement, viewership spikes, and commercial returns.

Think about it. When was the last time you tuned into India cricket score updates and got excited about a low-scoring, closely-fought match where the team defending 145 actually held their nerve? Compare that to the roar of the crowd when a batter smashes a six into the night sky. One feels like chess; the other feels like a spectacle.

Muralidaran's statement reflects a deliberate strategy: wickets that favor batters, shorter boundaries in some stadiums, and pace-friendly or spin-friendly pitches picked strategically aren't just coincidental. They're part of the formula. The IPL 2025 season has been a masterclass in this philosophy.

What Does This Mean for India Cricket in 2025-26?

For Indian diaspora fans in the US catching IPL 2025 highlights at midnight, this shift matters more than you think. Here's why:

  • You're not watching fair cricket—you're watching entertainment theater designed around commercial imperatives.
  • Test cricket suffers by comparison because it can't compete with the spectacle. India vs Australia Test series gets less hype than IPL drama.
  • Player development skews toward power-hitting over technique. Younger Indian cricketers are learning to hit sixes, not build innings.
  • Viewer expectations reset. When you watch 200+ run matches every week, international cricket feels "slow" and "boring."

The genius (or danger, depending on your perspective) is that Muralidaran acknowledges this trade-off openly. He's not pretending the IPL is pure sport—he's saying that's fine, because it's big business now. The moment you accept that, everything else makes sense.

The Pressure on Indian Cricket

Here's where it gets tricky for India cricket as a whole. The IPL's dominance means:

When international fixtures happen, they're often compared to IPL standards. India cricket score expectations have shifted upward. A 280-run total in a Test match feels underwhelming to fans raised on 200+ IPL games. The BCCI's scheduling decisions increasingly favor IPL windows. Domestic cricket? It gets whatever's left over.

Muralidaran isn't criticizing this—he's just naming it. And that clarity is refreshing, even if it's uncomfortable.

Fair Play vs. Spectacle: Where's the Balance?

The real question isn't whether Muralidaran is right (he obviously is, empirically). The question is: should we care?

If you're a cricket purist, this is deeply troubling. Fair wickets, balanced conditions, and unpredictable outcomes are what made cricket a gentleman's game. But if you're a casual viewer checking India cricket highlights on your lunch break, you probably want fireworks, not a four-hour grind.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Is Muralidaran saying the IPL is fixed or rigged?

A: No. He's saying the IPL deliberately designs playing conditions (pitch, boundaries, weather windows) to favor entertainment and high-scoring matches. That's not cheating—it's strategic business. Think of it like how WWE designs storylines vs. boxing's competitive fairness. Both are legitimate entertainment products, just different philosophies.

Q: Does this mean India cricket international matches are also rigged?

A: Not necessarily. International cricket (India vs Australia Tests, ODIs, etc.) operates under different governance. But the IPL 2025's success is influencing how BCCI thinks about all cricket—including pitch preparation for international games. The influence is real, but it's not identical.

Q: Why should I care about fair wickets if I'm just watching for entertainment?

A: You probably shouldn't! If you're a casual IPL 2025 fan looking for India cricket highlights with sixes and drama, fair play is irrelevant. But if you care about skill, technique, and competitive integrity—especially for players developing young talent—then it matters deeply. It's a values question, not a facts question.

The bottom line? Muralidaran just articulated what cricket insiders have known for years: the IPL isn't cricket in its purest form anymore. It's entertainment. And that's working beautifully for the business. Whether that's good or bad depends on what you want from the game.
