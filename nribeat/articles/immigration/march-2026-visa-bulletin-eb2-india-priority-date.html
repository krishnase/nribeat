March 2026 Visa Bulletin: EB-2 India Priority Date Update

📅 March 24, 2026 ⏱ 5 min read ✍️ NRIBeat Editorial Source: travel.state.gov ↗
Disclosure: NRIBeat may earn a commission from links in this article at no extra cost to you. We only recommend services we believe in.

The March 2026 Visa Bulletin has been released, and if you're on the EB-2 employment-based green card track from India, here's what you need to know: the EB-2 India priority date is currently frozen at November 1, 2014. This means applicants with priority dates on or before this date may be moving toward visa availability, while those with later dates will continue to wait. With over 800,000 Indians in the green card backlog, understanding where your priority date stands is critical to planning your immigration timeline in 2026.

What You Need to Know

  • EB-2 India Priority Date (Final): November 1, 2014 — no movement from the previous month
  • EB-1 India Priority Date (Final): December 1, 2023 — continues to advance for priority workers and researchers
  • EB-3 India Priority Date (Final): August 15, 2014 — the oldest priority date category, still in backlog
  • What "Final" means: These are the cutoff dates; only applicants with priority dates on or before these dates can proceed to the next stage of green card processing
  • Impact on USCIS processing time: Visa bulletin delays directly affect how long your case remains in queue before adjustment of status or consular processing

EB-2 India Priority Date Stalled: What Does This Mean?

The EB-2 India category has been one of the most heavily impacted employment-based green card routes. With the priority date holding steady at November 1, 2014, we're seeing a stall in movement—a sign that visa numbers are being exhausted quickly or that the allocation for India in the March 2026 cycle is constrained.

For context: if your EB-2 priority date is November 1, 2014 or earlier, you're in the "current" zone and may receive an Adjustment of Status (AOS) or Consular Processing (CP) interview notice relatively soon. However, if your priority date falls after November 1, 2014, you remain in visa backlog status, and your green card processing will extend years into the future—a phenomenon known as the "per-country limit" under U.S. immigration law.

India accounts for approximately 60% of all employment-based green card applicants globally. With only 140,000 employment-based visas issued annually across all categories and countries, the demand far exceeds supply. This creates the backlog that Indian tech workers, doctors, engineers, and other skilled professionals face today.

EB-1 and EB-3 Categories: Where They Stand in March 2026

EB-1 (Priority Workers): The EB-1 India priority date has advanced to December 1, 2023—a significant improvement. This category includes multinational executives, outstanding researchers, and individuals with extraordinary ability. If you qualify for EB-1 and your priority date is on or before December 1, 2023, you should see movement on your USCIS processing within the next 4–8 months.

EB-3 (Skilled Workers & Professionals): The EB-3 India priority date remains at August 15, 2014—even older than EB-2. This suggests that EB-3 cases are moving even slower than EB-2, reflecting the immense backlog in the unskilled and semi-skilled worker categories. If you filed an EB-3 petition after 2015, you should expect a 10+ year wait before visa availability.

Understanding Priority Dates vs. Visa Availability

A common confusion among applicants: having a priority date doesn't mean you have a green card offer. Your priority date is simply your place in line. The actual "Visa Bulletin" published monthly by the State Department tells you which priority dates are currently "current" (meaning visas are available for those dates).

The March 2026 bulletin indicates that only applicants with EB-2 priority dates of November 1, 2014 or earlier can move forward. Everyone else waits. This is why tracking each monthly bulletin is essential—even a small advance of a few months or weeks signals progress and helps you estimate when your own case might reach the finish line.

What This Means for You

If you're an Indian H-1B holder or OPT graduate on the EB-2 track, the March 2026 visa bulletin reveals a stalled category. For those with priority dates before November 1, 2014: your case is actively moving; plan to finalize medical exams, police certificates, and interview preparation within the next 6–12 months. For those with priority dates after November 1, 2014: continue building your career, maintain H-1B or L-1 status (or explore green card recapture), and prepare for a multi-year wait. The good news: continuing to file extensions keeps you current and eligible once your turn arrives.

For EB-3 applicants: the August 15, 2014 cutoff means a significant portion of Indian EB-3 filers (post-2010) won't see movement until 2028 or beyond. Consider whether recapturing unused years of H-1B time or exploring EB-1C (multinational executive) options might accelerate your timeline.

Step-by-Step: What to Do Now

Step 1: Check Your Priority Date

Locate your I-797 approval notice or USCIS online account. Your priority date is listed in Section 2 of your I-140 approval. Compare it to the March 2026 bulletin dates above.

Step 2: Determine Your Status

If your priority date is on or before the cutoff, you're "current." If it's after, you're in backlog. USCIS processing time for current cases is typically 1–2 years for AOS or CP interviews.

Step 3: Prepare Required Documents

Current applicants should gather: medical exam results (Form I-693), police clearance certificate, birth and marriage certificates, passport, and employment letters. These typically take 2–3 months to collect.

Step 4: Monitor Monthly Bulletins

Subscribe to the State Department's visa bulletin or set alerts on NRIBeat. Even small advances signal progress. The April 2026 bulletin will be released in mid-April.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does "EB-2 priority date" mean?

Your priority date is the date your labor certification was filed (or in some cases, when your I-140 was filed). It determines your position in the green card queue. The State Department uses priority dates to allocate visa numbers monthly. Only applicants at or before the current "cutoff" priority date can move forward in processing.

How long does USCIS processing time take after my priority date becomes current?

Once your priority date is current (meets the visa bulletin cutoff), USCIS typically processes green card applications within 12–24 months. This includes background checks, interviews, and final approval. However, delays at USCIS field offices can extend this to 2–3 years in some cases. Check your local field office processing times on USCIS.gov.

Will the EB-2 India priority date advance in the coming months?

Movement depends on visa number allocation and demand. Historically, EB-2 India has advanced 6–12 months per year, but this is unpredictable. If Congress increases the employment-based visa cap or removes the per-country limit, movement could accelerate significantly. For now, assume the November 1, 2014 date may hold steady or advance slowly through 2026.

Conclusion

The March 2026 Visa Bulletin shows that EB-2 India remains stalled at November 1, 2014, with EB-1 making progress and EB-3 lagging further behind. If you're in the current zone, take action immediately—collect documents and prepare for your interview. If you're in backlog, use these years wisely: strengthen your employer sponsorship, consider alternative visa categories, or explore policy advocacy. Immigration timelines are unpredictable, but staying informed and prepared puts you ahead.
