Reasoning Behind the Prediction

1. Seasonal Pattern Analysis

May falls within the April-June period, historically characterized by steady but moderate movement in the Visa Bulletin. This quarter typically sees:

  • Consistent forward progression without dramatic jumps
  • Average monthly movement of 10-14 days for EB-2 India in normal conditions
  • Stable visa number allocation as the fiscal year progresses (FY2026 runs through September 2026)

We expect May to follow this pattern with moderate movement rather than the outsized 304-day jump seen in December 2025 (which was likely a data correction or category recapture event).

Removing the anomalous December spike, the normalized pattern is clear: 14-16 days per month. This consistency suggests the system has stabilized after the large correction, and we should expect similar modest progression.

3. Historical 12-Month Average

EB-2 India averaged 72.7 days per month during positive movement months. However, this figure is heavily influenced by the December outlier. The recent 6-month window (14-16 days) is more reliable for near-term forecasting and aligns with typical April-June behavior.

4. Fiscal Year Considerations

With FY2026 still having 4+ months remaining (through September 2026), visa number allocation should remain steady. There is no immediate pressure to retrogress or dramatically accelerate—the USCIS has demonstrated capacity to maintain regular monthly progression.

5. EB-3 India Comparison

EB-3 India (Final Action Date: November 15, 2013) is only 8 months behind EB-2 and averaging 43.4 days/month. The smaller EB-3 movement suggests visa number constraints are manageable for both categories, supporting modest but consistent EB-2 progress.

Retrogression Risk Assessment: LOW (5-10%)

Why Retrogression is Unlikely in May 2026

  • Fiscal Year Runway: 5+ months remain in FY2026 (through September 30), providing adequate time to allocate visa numbers without scrambling
  • Current Trajectory: At 15 days/month, reaching FY2026 end without retrogression is achievable
  • No External Pressures: No indication of sudden employment-based visa number reductions or legislative changes as of April 2026
  • Historical Pattern: Retrogression typically occurs July-September when fiscal year quota exhaustion becomes imminent. May is too early in the cycle

Retrogression Risk Window

Watch for retrogression risk in July-September 2026 (end of fiscal year), not May. If USCIS fails to clear the backlog at current pace, slowdown or retrogression could occur in late summer.

What Could Change This Prediction?

Upside Scenarios (Larger Movement)

  • Congress approves employment-based visa number increase mid-year
  • Unused visa numbers from other categories (EB-1, EB-4, EB-5) are recaptured to EB-2/EB-3
  • USCIS accelerates case processing due to policy changes

Downside Scenarios (Smaller Movement)

  • Unexpected surge in new EB-2 India applications reduces forward progress
  • Administrative processing delays slow case adjudication
  • Legislative action reduces available visa numbers for employment-based immigration

Implications for Indian EB-2 Applicants

If our prediction holds:

  • Your Current Priority Date: If filed between July 15-28, 2014, you may become current (eligible to file I-485) in May 2026
  • Near-Misses: If your priority date is August 2014, you're likely 1-2 months away from becoming current
  • Backlogs (Pre-2014): The backlog remains severe at 11+ years, requiring continued patience

Comparison with EB-3 India

EB-3 India's Final Action Date at November 15, 2013 (8 months behind EB-2) is moving slower in absolute terms (43 days/month vs. 72 days/month), meaning EB-3 applicants should expect proportionally slower movement toward currency.

Bottom Line

May 2026 should bring modest, steady progress for EB-2 India with 12-18 days of forward movement, taking the Final Action Date to late July 2014. While the years-long backlog remains daunting, the predictable monthly progression offers hope that the bottleneck is slowly clearing without dramatic year-end retrogression risks.
